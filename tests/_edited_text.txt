[P0032] Abstract
[P0033] BACKGROUND
[P0034] Coffee consumption exhibits remarkable regional disparities, with a substantial gap in coffee intake observed between China and European/American countries. However, there are few studies on coffee, coffee with milk, glucose metabolism and diabetes, and most of them are done in European and American countries.
[P0036] AIM
[P0037] To explore the relationship between coffee consumption, diabetes and glucose indices in Chinese adults.
[P0039] METHODS
[P0040] A cross-sectional survey with community participation was conducted in Guangzhou, China, and 6876 subjects were included. These participants provided complete records of their coffee consumption and coffee additives (milk, sugar, artificial sweeteners) over the past year. The diabetic indicators [glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c), fasting plasma glucose, and 2-hour blood glucose (2h-PG)] were measured using the oral glucose tolerance test method. Statistical analyses included multivariable logistic and subgroup analyses.
[P0042] RESULTS
[P0043] After adjusting the influence of some other factors, we found that the chances of drinking coffee and diabetes are inversely related [odds ratio (OR) = 0.79, 95% confidence interval (CI): 0.64-0.98, P = 0.029], and the high level of 2h-PG is also inversely related (OR = 0.83, 95%CI: 0.73-0.95, P = 0.005). For those who drink coffee with milk, the chance of high 2h-PG level is reduced by 24% (OR = 0.76, 95%CI: 0.66-0.88, P < 0.001), and the chance of high HbA1c is reduced by 28% (OR = 0.72, 95%CI: 0.63-0.83, P < 0.001).
[P0045] CONCLUSION
[P0046] Coffee intake exhibits a negative correlation with the prevalence of diabetes, elevated 2h-PG, and elevated HbA1c levels. Milk coffee was inversely associated with diabetes, elevated 2h-PG and elevated HbA1c.

With relatively few relevant studies in China compared to European and American countries, results showed coffee and milk-added coffee were negatively correlated with diabetes, elevated two-hour postprandial blood glucose, and elevated glycated hemoglobin levels.
[P0054] INTRODUCTION
[P0055] Impaired glucose metabolism, the hallmark of diabetes, contributes to systemic complications and elevated prevalence of premature death[1,2]. In 2022, the proportion of adults in the world who have diabetes is 13.9% among women and 14.3% among men[3]. China’s diabetes-related expenditures are projected to rise to 460.4 billion dollars by 2030, nearly doubling from a decade ago[4]. Of great significance is the search for factors in diabetes prevention and intervention. The role of lifestyle changes such as dietary interventions is crucial in controlling blood sugar levels[5-7]. Drinking coffee is related to our prevalence of type 2 diabetes. Previous studies have shown that the more coffee we drink, the lower the prevalence of disease[8,9].
[P0056] Globally, coffee stands as one of the most commonly consumed drinks[10,11]. Since 2012, the global coffee consumption has been growing at an annual rate of 2%. It is worth noting that the average consumption rate in the Asian market stands at 5.2%, with the most significant growth observed in recent years[12]. There are many good things in coffee, such as chlorogenic acid and caffeine, which can help our bodies to deal with sugar better[13-15]. There are many good things in coffee, such as chlorogenic acids and caffeine, which can resist oxidation and inflammation. Previous studies have found that coffee can also help our bodies deal with sugar and fat better[16-18]. Studies have indicated that adding milk to coffee can enhance its bioaccessible[19]. Drinking latte or cappuccino-coffee with milk-can help us stay away from metabolic syndrome and high blood pressure, but drinking espresso has no such benefits[20]. As coffee consumption increases, the prevalence of type 2 diabetes decreases progressively[14,15,20]. Many studies have shown that the intake of beverages containing sugar or artificial sweeteners is positively correlated with the prevalence of diabetes[21,22].
[P0057] Notably, coffee consumption exhibits remarkable regional disparities, with a substantial gap in coffee intake observed between China and European/American countries. However, there are few studies on the relationship between coffee, coffee varieties added with milk and diabetes, and most of these studies are done in European and American countries. Therefore, we need to do more research and explore the relationship between drinking coffee, the types of coffee, diabetes and glucose metabolism from different angles. We want to study whether there is a relationship between drinking coffee (including adding milk to black coffee) and diabetes, high glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c), high fasting plasma glucose (FPG), and high 2-hour blood glucose (2h-PG) in China community.
[P0059] MATERIALS AND METHODS
[P0060] Study design and subjects
[P0061] Between November 2021 and September 2022, a community cohort study called the “Shunde Metabolic Diseases and Risk Factors Epidemiological Study” was conducted in China. The representativeness of our research samples has been effectively guaranteed by multi-stage stratified random sampling technology[23]. In this study, all participants signed informed consent forms.
[P0062] From all our volunteers, 3412 people were not counted because they didn’t have blood tests. Participants with missing coffee consumption data or those who consumed coffee with added sugar and artificial sweeteners were excluded from the analysis (n = 2350), those with a prior diagnosis of diabetes (n = 602). There are 295 people with incomplete information, such as their gender, age, education level, body mass index (BMI), smoking, physical activity and drinking. We have incomplete data. According to the selection and exclusion rules mentioned above, we selected a total of 6876 people in this study (Figure 1).
[P0064] Data collection
[P0065] Structured questionnaires were administered by trained medical staff, covering aspects such as demographic information (including age, educational attainment, and gender), lifestyle habits (like smoking behavior and work - related physical activity), as well as medication and medical histories. Subsequently, a physical assessment was carried out, during which weight and height were measured. A person’s weight (kg) divided by the square of his height (meter) can calculate his BMI[24].
[P0066] After 10 hours of fasting, we asked everyone to draw about 30 mL of blood from the blood vessels in their arms between 7 am and 10 am on the same day. The anticoagulant-treated vacuum tube samples were promptly transferred to a College of American Pathologists-certified icebox and processed within two hours of collection, undergoing centrifugation, aliquoting, and storage at -20 °C. Additionally, if participants self-reported a diabetes diagnosis during the survey, further tests, including HbA1c and FPG, were conducted. Plasma glucose was measured 2 hours after 75-gram oral glucose tolerance test for those who did not have a clear diagnosis of diabetes[25].
[P0067] Glycosylated hemoglobin was detected by high performance liquid chromatography (instrument model HLC-723G8, Tosoh Corporation, Japan). For clinical biochemical testing, venous blood samples were collected after subjects had fasted for at least 10 hours. Samples were immediately centrifuged and sent to a central laboratory certified by external quality assessment. A Beckman Coulter, Inc. (CA, United States) AU5831 automatic biochemical analyzer was used to detect FPG, 2h-PG, total cholesterol (TC), triglycerides (TG), the concentration of high-density lipoprotein cholesterol (HDL-C), and the concentration of low-density lipoprotein cholesterol (LDL-C)[29].
[P0068] According to the provisions of the 2024 American Diabetes Association, if a person met at least one of the following conditions, it could be determined that they had diabetes: FPG ≥ 7.0 mmol/L, 2h-PG ≥ 11.1 mmol/L, HbA1c ≥ 6.5%, or they stated that they were using medicine to manage diabetes. Therefore, the definition of high FPG was ≥ 5.6 mmol/L, that of high 2h-PG was ≥ 7.8 mmol/L, and that of high HbA1c was ≥ 5.7%[26]. According to the guideline of the International Society of Hypertension in 2020, hypertension referred to systolic blood pressure ≥ 140 mmHg, or diastolic blood pressure ≥ 90 mmHg, or they had been diagnosed with hypertension or were taking antihypertensive drugs[27]. Dyslipidemia referred to any of the following conditions: TC ≥ 6.22 mmol/L, TG ≥ 2.26 mmol/L, LDL-C ≥ 4.14 mmol/L, HDL-C < 1.04 mmol/L, or they stated that they had been diagnosed with dyslipidemia by a doctor[23]. In addition, those who say they are taking cholesterol-lowering drugs are also counted as having hyperlipidemia problems.
[P0069] According to the demographic survey results, the educational level is categorized into the following three groups: Those who have received education below high school level, those who have obtained a high school diploma, and those who have attained education beyond high school level[28]. Age was divided into two groups: < 60 years and ≥ 60 years[29]. BMI is divided into two groups: BMI < 24.0 kg/m2 and BMI ≥ 24.0 kg/m2[25]. The standard definitions of smoking status are as follows: Non-smokers, former smokers (individuals who have smoked but have abstained for more than 6 months), and current smokers (individuals who currently smoke and have consumed a total of more than 100 cigarettes)[30]. Alcohol consumption is categorized into drinkers and non-drinkers. Occupational physical activity was evaluated using a closed-ended questionnaire with four predefined categories: Sedentary (primarily sitting), moderate (repetitive movements while seated or standing, typical in manufacturing work), vigorous (primarily standing or walking, common in construction and agricultural jobs), and heavy (involving lifting, loading or moving heavy objects)[31]. First-degree relatives of patients with diabetes was self-reported through a personal family history health questionnaire, and categorized into two groups: Yes, no.
[P0071] Coffee consumption
[P0072] Basic information about coffee consumption was collected by trained medical staff. In this study, we used a validated “24-hour dietary recall questionnaire” to find out how much coffee people drank and what was added to the coffee[32]. Participants were required to report how much coffee they drank within 24 hours, and indicate in the questionnaire whether other things (such as milk, sugar, artificial sweeteners) had been added to the coffee. We defined one serving of coffee as 150 milliliters. In the analysis, non-coffee drinkers were defined as individuals who consumed coffee less than once. Considering the generally low coffee consumption among the Chinese population, we simplified the classification criteria and divided the participants into two groups: Non-consumers and daily consumers (≥ 1 cup). Coffee drinkers also reported whether they added milk to their coffee. In this way, we divided the participants into three categories: Those who did not drink coffee, those who only drank black coffee, and those who drank coffee with milk[33].
[P0074] Statistical analysis
[P0075] This cross-sectional observation and analysis shows that the total data loss rate of all variables in our model is 4.29%. Considering that there are few missing data (≤ 5%), and in order to avoid the possible deviation caused by estimation method, we directly analyze the complete data. Only those participants who have all the information are included in the final analysis. Baseline comparisons were performed between the pre-exclusion group (including participants with missing coffee intake data, missing covariate data, or those consuming coffee containing added sugar) and the complete case group after exclusion. No significant differences were observed across key variables, indicating minimal selection bias in the present study.
[P0076] We did a study and used a method called binary logic regression. We want to see the difference between people who drink coffee and those who don’t drink coffee, the prevalence of diabetes and their blood sugar levels. Finally, we calculated the odds ratio (OR) and its 95% confidence interval (CI) to show the strength of this correlation. We built two models to adjust different things. Model 1 includes age, gender and BMI; in model 2, in addition to those in model 1, education level, smoking or not, exercise or not, drinking or not, hypertension, TC, TG, LDL-C, HDL-C, and whether there is diabetes in the family are added, which are variables used for examination. Our survey also studied the relationship between coffee drinking habits, diabetes and glucose metabolism.
[P0077] We also conducted additional exploratory stratified analysis according to age was divided into two groups: < 60 years and ≥ 60 years, BMI (less than 24 and over 24), gender (female, male), education level (primary school, middle school, university), physical activity (no exercise, low intensity exercise, moderate intensity exercise, high intensity exercise) and hypertension (with or without). By excluding people with chronic diseases (hypertension and hyperlipidemia), we made a more careful analysis. For the sensitivity analysis, the same statistical models (model 1 and model 2) as the primary analysis were employed, with the only modification being the exclusion of the “hypertension” variable from the covariate list. P < 0.05 is defined as significant statistical difference. All the analysis was done with SPSS version 26.
[P0079] RESULTS
[P0080] Characteristics of participants
[P0081] Among the 6876 subjects who finally entered the queue, 25.00% said that they would drink a cup of coffee every day. Among them, 11.94% of the participants were diagnosed with diabetes. 86.65% were non-smokers. 62.65% of all participants were female. Table 1 shows the basic situation of people who drink coffee and those who don’t. In the comparison between the two groups, in the group who drank coffee, there were fewer smokers and fewer people with less than high school education. The proportion of people diagnosed with hypertension is relatively low.
[P0083] Coffee consumption amount, prevalence of diabetes, and indicators of blood glucose regulation
[P0084] Including the ORs of diabetes and glucose metabolism parameters adjusted by coffee drinking status (Table 2). In model 2, we found that coffee consumption was negatively associated with diabetes (OR = 0.79, 95%CI: 0.64-0.98, P = 0.029), high 2h-PG (OR = 0.83, 95%CI: 0.73-0.95, P = 0.005), high HbA1c levels(OR = 0.76, 95%CI: 0.67-0.86, P < 0.001).
[P0086] Correlation analysis of coffee types
[P0087] The results of the correlation analysis for different coffee types are summarized in Table 3. In model 2, we found that compared with participants who did not drink coffee, those who drank coffee with milk had a 24% lower prevalence of high 2h-PG (OR = 0.76, 95%CI: 0.66-0.88, P < 0.001), a 28% lower prevalence of high HbA1c levels (OR = 0.72, 95%CI: 0.63-0.83, P < 0.001) and a 25% reduction in the prevalence of diabetes (OR = 0.75, 95%CI: 0.59-0.95, P = 0.017).

[P0090] In all the groups, we didn’t find any connection between drinking coffee and diabetes (Table 4), including age, BMI, gender, educational attainment, and physical activity, and hypertension (interaction P > 0.05).

[P0093] We performed a sensitivity analysis in which participants with hypertension and hyperlipidemia were excluded, and the corresponding results are presented in Table 5. After excluding participants with hypertension (n = 1708) and hyperlipidemia (n = 1544), the findings remained substantially consistent with those of the primary analysis. Our analysis revealed an inverse association between coffee consumption and elevated high HbA1c levels (OR = 0.76, 95%CI: 0.64-0.91, P = 0.002), diabetes (OR = 0.68, 95%CI: 0.47-0.99, P = 0.045), and elevated 2h-PG levels (OR= 0.83, 95%CI: 0.70-0.99, P = 0.035).
[P0095] DISCUSSION
[P0096] Coffee intake exhibits a negative correlation with the prevalence of diabetes, elevated 2h-PG, and elevated HbA1c levels. Milk coffee was inversely associated with diabetes, elevated 2h-PG and elevated HbA1c. Several previous studies have reported an association between coffee consumption and the risk of type 2 diabetes[33,34]. Unlike numerous foreign studies that classify coffee intake into multiple cup-specific groups based on local consumption patterns, we simplified the categorization into two groups, non-drinkers (0 cups/day) and daily consumers of 1 or more cups, due to the overall low coffee consumption among the Chinese population. Importantly, drinking coffee is still associated with a reduced prevalence of diabetes in this group. Similarly, a study following three large cohorts of American men and women over many years found that those consuming coffee had an 11% lower prevalence of diabetes[35]. An analysis of 30 studies found that drinking an extra cup of coffee every day can reduce the prevalence of type 2 diabetes by 6%[20]. A review analyzing 8 selected clinical trials (involving 2447 subjects) found that increasing coffee intake for at least two weeks improved glucose metabolism[36]. The conclusions of previous observational studies are consistent with those of this study.
[P0097] The association between coffee consumption and diabetes is mediated by several mechanisms. First, coffee influences lipid metabolism and energy expenditure. Specifically, it promotes hepatic fat oxidation[37]. Given the established link between hepatic steatosis, metabolic dysfunction, and type 2 diabetes, coffee exerts a regulatory effect on metabolic homeostasis[38]. Coffee also elevates the body’s core temperature; this process enhances glycemic control by activating brown adipose tissue to increase energy expenditure[39,40].
[P0098] Our study revealed that, in stark contrast to those who opted for black coffee, individuals who regularly consumed coffee with milk experienced a much more significant reduction in prevalence of diabetes. A study has proposed that polyphenols rich in coffee, primarily caffeic acid, chlorogenic acid[41], can form complex bonds through covalent linkages with essential milk proteins like cysteine. This unique combination allows them to maintain a highly stable configuration[42].
[P0099] This study has the following main limitations: First, coffee intake was measured using dietary questionnaires. However, dietary assessments are susceptible to error, which may compromise the accuracy of our research findings. Additionally, we have proposed conducting a validation sub-study in future research to address this methodological consideration. Second, we did not obtain detailed information such as caffeine intake, or brewing method, which may limit the depth of the analysis. Notably, decaffeinated coffee is uncommon in China. These factors are closely associated with coffee consumption. Future research will take these problems into account, so that we can know more clearly what the relationship between drinking coffee and diabetes is, and we will also pay attention to excluding other factors that may cause trouble. Third, the present study is that the sample was restricted to Han Chinese individuals residing in Shunde City, Guangdong Province, China. Therefore, this result may not be applicable to people in other parts of China, nor to members of different ethnic groups. Fourthly, among the China participants in our study, only a few people drank more than 2 cups of coffee every day. Consequently, coffee intake could not be treated as a continuous variable based on cup count. Instead, we dichotomized coffee intake into two categories: “Non-consumers” and “≥ 1 cup/day”. This method makes the dose-response relationship simpler and more direct, and future research can analyze coffee consumption as a continuous variable, so as to study the relationship between coffee consumption and diabetes more deeply. Fifth, although we have adjusted important related factors in the model, we still can’t completely eliminate the residual interference, which may be because some factors have not been measured or the measured variables have not been adjusted perfectly. Another limitation of cross-sectional study is that it cannot determine the causal relationship between variables. Future research can use randomized controlled trials and explore the mechanism behind it to verify the results of our investigation.
[P0101] CONCLUSION
[P0102] Coffee intake exhibits a negative correlation with the prevalence of diabetes, elevated 2h-PG, and elevated HbA1c levels. Milk coffee was inversely associated with diabetes, elevated 2h-PG and elevated HbA1c. Although this connection doesn’t seem particularly strong, we still need to do more verification through randomized controlled trials or studying the principle behind it.
